1 Review: Gauge theory

2 Spin geometry

As illustrated by the Dirac's belt trick (imagine slices of the belt as many instance of the first slice through a loop in \( SO(3) \)), the fundamental group \( \pi_1(SO(n)) \) of the special orthogonal group is \( \mathbb{Z}/_{2 \mathbb{Z}} \). Its universal cover is hence a double cover and therefore a compact simply-connected Lie group, called the Spin group. It is useful to see the Spin group as a subset of Clifford algebra, which is the exterior algebra with multiplication modified, and study their representations as representations of the latter. It turns out that when \( n=2m \) is even, Clifford algebra has a unique complex irreducible representation \( S \) of dimension \( 2^m \), that splits into two, says \( S= S_+ \oplus S_- \) as representation of the Spin group.

The frame bundle of an \( n \)-dimensional Riemannian manifold \( X \) is a principal \( SO(n) \) bundle over \( X \). If this bundle can be lifted to a \( \Spin(n) \) bundle, the manifold is said to be a Spin manifold. Suppose \( n=2m \), one can construct

  1. the associated vector bundles \( S_{\pm}(X) \), called Spinor bundles, using the representations \( S_{\pm} \) of Spin. These are hermitian vector bundles because one can choose a hermitian metric on \( S_\pm \) that is invariant by action of the (compact) Spin group.
  2. the Clifford bundle using action of Spin group on Clifford algebra by conjugation. This is, as a vector bundle, just the exterior product \( \bigwedge^\bullet TM \) of tangent bundle, but the action by multiplication of an element of Clifford algebra on elements of \( S_{\pm} \) gives a well-defined action of a point of Clifford bundle on Spinor bundles \( S_{\pm}(X) \) that sends one to the other. This is called Clifford multiplication.

The (Ehresmann) connection given by pulling back the Levi-Civita connection from the frame bundle to the Spin bundle (its double cover) induces a connection on \( S_{\pm}(X) \): \[ \nabla: \Gamma(S_\pm(X)) \longrightarrow \Gamma(TX^*\otimes S_{\pm(X)}) \] Contracting using Clifford multiplication (and identifying \( TX^* \) with \( TX \) using the Riemannian metric), one obtains the Dirac operator \[ \not{\partial}: \Gamma(S_\pm(X)) \longrightarrow \Gamma(S_\mp(X)) \]

One can repeat the previous construction for the complex Spin group \( \Spin^c(n) = \Spin(n)\times_{\pm}U(1) \) which is a double cover of \( SO(n)\times U(1) \) and is not \( \Spin(n)\times U(1) \): \[ \xymatrix{ \Spin(n)\times U(1) \ar@{->}[r]^{2:1} \ar@{->}[d]_{2:1} & SO(n)\times U(1) \ar@{->}[d]^{2:1} \\ \Spin^c(n) \ar@{->}[r]^{2:1} & SO(n)\times U(1) } \] Given a \( \Spin^c \) principal bundle the bottom-right \( U(1) \) gives a hermitian (complex) line bundle \( L \) where one will need a connection \( A \), in addition to the Levi-Civita connection on the \( SO(n) \) factor, in order to define Dirac operator \( \not{\partial_A} \) on spinors. The advantage is that \( \Spin^c \) structure always exists in four-dimensional manifolds.

2.1 Spin group and spin representation

Let \( (V,g) \) be a \( \mathbb{R} \)-vector space equiped with a metric of dimension \( n \). We will construct its Clifford algebra \(\Cl (V) \) and define the spin group \( \Spin(V) \) as the group generated by element of \( \Cl(V) \) with unit norm and pair degree. We will then see that there is only one irreducible complex representation of \( \Cl (V)\otimes \mathbb{C} \) when \( n=2m \) (two irreducible representations if \( n=2m+1 \)). These representations induces reducible representations of the corresponding Spin group and their decomposition will also be studied.

2.1.1 Clifford algebra

The Clifford algebra \( \Cl (V) \) of \( V \) is defined as a quotient of its tensor algebra \( T(V)=\bigoplus_{k=0}^\infty V^{\otimes k} \): \[ \Cl(V) := {T(V)}/_{(v\otimes v + |v|^2)} = \Cl_0(V)\oplus\Cl_1(V) \] which is a \( ^\mathbb{Z}/_{2 \mathbb{Z}} \)-graded algebra where even degree elements live in \( \Cl_1(V) \) and odd degree elements lives in \( \Cl_1(V) \).

We also denote by \( \Cl(n) \) the algebra structure of \( \Cl(V) \) for any \( n \)-dimensional normed vector space \( V \).

Let \( \{e_i\}\) be an orthonormal basis of \( V \), then

  1. \( \Cl (V) \) is generated by \( e_{i_1}\dots e_{i_k} \) for \( k=0,1,\dots,n \), hence \( \dim_{\mathbb{R}}\Cl(V) = 2^n \) is also the dimension of exterior algebra \( \bigwedge^\bullet V \) of \( V \).
  2. As \( \mathbb{R} \)-algebra, \( \Cl_0(V\oplus \mathbb{R}) \) is isomorphic to \( \Cl(V) \) via \[ \Cl(V) \ni v_0 + v_1 \longmapsto v_0 + v_1 e_{n+1}\in \Cl_0(V\oplus \mathbb{R)} \] where \( e_{n+1} \) is the extra unit vector of \( V\oplus \mathbb{R} \).

By dimension, \( \Cl (V) \) and \( \bigwedge^\bullet V \) are isomorphic as \( \mathbb{R} \)-vector spaces. We can, in addition, have a canonical linear (but not multiplicative) map \( \Cl(V) \longrightarrow \bigwedge^\bullet V \) given by reducing the identity map on \( T(V) \). This is because there is a natural linear (not multiplicative) map from \( \Cl(V) \) to its associated graded algebra corresponding to the filtration \( \mathcal{F}_k = \{ v\in Cl(V): \exists u\in \bigoplus_{i=0^k}V^{\otimes i}\subset T(V) \text{ representing } v\}, k=0,..,n \) and the following fact.

Let \( \mathcal{F}_0\subset \mathcal{F}_1\subset\dots \) be the filtration of \( \Cl(V) \) given by the filtration of \( T(V) \) by degree. Then \[ {\rm Gr}_{\mathcal{F}_\bullet} \simeq \bigwedge^\bullet(V) \] as \( \mathbb{R} \)-algebra.

Let \( \widetilde{\mathcal{F}_\bullet} \) be the filtration of \( \bigwedge^\bullet(V) \) given by the filtration of \( T(V) \) by degree, then \( \widetilde{\mathcal{F}_k}/_{\widetilde{\mathcal{F}_{k-1}}} \simeq \mathcal{F}_k/_{\mathcal{F}_{k-1}} \).

  1. The Pin group \( \Pin(V) \) is the subgroup of \( \Cl^\times(V) \) generated by elements \(v\in V \) with \( |v|=1 \).
  2. The Spin group \( \Spin(V):=\Pin(V)\cap \Cl_0(V) \) is the subgroup of index 2 of Pin group containing elements of even degree.

2.1.2 \( \Spin(n) \) as the universal cover of \( SO(n) \)

the construction of Clifford algebra is unique in the sense that any isometry \( h: V_1 \stackrel{\simeq}{\longrightarrow} V_2 \) can be extended to an \( \mathbb{R} \)-algebra isomorphism of Clifford algebras. \[ \xymatrix{ \Cl(V_1) \ar@{->}[r]^{H} & \Cl(V_2) \\ V_1 \ar@{->}[r]^{h} \ar@{^{(}->}[u] & V_2 \ar@{^{(}->}[u] } \]

The first consequence of Remark rem:unique-cl is that any isometry of \( V \) induces a \( \mathbb{R} \)-algebra automorphism. The other direction also holds.

Any automorphism of \( \Cl(V) \) as \( \mathbb{R} \)-algebra preserving \( V \) as a whole is induced by a unique element of \( O(V) \). Therefore \( O(V) \) is in 1:1 correspondence with the set of \( \mathbb{R} \)-algebra automorphisms of \( \Cl(V) \) preserving \( V \).

So one can associate an isometry of \( V \) to each element of \( \Pin(V) \) via its action by conjugation.

  1. The action by conjugation of \( \Pin(V) \) on \( \Cl(V) \) gives a group morphism \( \Pin(V) \longrightarrow O(V) \) and \( \Spin(V) \longrightarrow SO(V) \). The image of \( \Pin(V) \) in \( O(V) \) contains all reflections. Hence these two morphisms are surjective,
  2. The kernel of \( \Spin(V) \longrightarrow SO(V) \) is the intersection with the center of \( \Cl(V) \), hence, is \( \{\pm 1\} \). In other words, \( \Spin(V) \) is a double cover of \( SO(V) \).
  3. \( \Spin(V) \) is connected for any \( n=\dim V \geq 2 \) and \( \pi_1(SO(V)) = \mathbb{Z}/2 \mathbb{Z} \) for any \( n\geq 3 \).Hence \( \Spin(V) \) is the universal cover of \( SO(V) \) if the dimension of \( V \) is at least 3.

For the first 2 claims, note that conjugation by \( v\in V\subset \Cl(V) \) is the reflection by the hyperplane \( v=0 \).

To see that \( \Spin(V) \) is connected and hence not the trivial \( 2:1 \) cover of \( SO(V) \), note that \[ -\cos(\theta_2-\theta_1) e_1 + \sin(\theta_2 -\theta_1) e_2 = (\cos\theta_1 e_1 + \sin\theta_1 e_2)(\cos\theta_2 e_1 + \sin\theta_2 e_2)\in\Spin(V), \] so \( \pm 1 \) can be connected by a path.

The second consequence of Remark rem:unique-cl is that: if \( h:V_1 \stackrel{\simeq}{\longrightarrow} V_2 \) is isometry then the induced the adjoint map \( Ad(h): SO(V_1) \longrightarrow SO(V_2) \) can be lifted to Spin groups and the following \( h^\# \) is just the restriction of \( h^\# \) on \( \Cl(V_1)\supset \Spin(V_1) \) \[ \xymatrix{ \Spin(V_1) \ar@{->}[r]^{h^\#} \ar@{->}[d] & \Spin(V_2) \ar@{->}[d] \\ SO(V_1) \ar@{->}[r]^{Ad(h)} & SO(V_2) } \] In particular (take \( V_1=V_2) \), this says that the action of \( SO(V) \) on itself by conjugation can be lifted to \( \Spin(V) \). Same story with \( \Pin(V) \longrightarrow O(V) \).

Let \( (e_1,\dots, e_n) \) be an oriented orthonormal basis of \( V \), then the element \[ q:=e_1\dots e_n\in \Pin(V)\subset\Cl(V) \] is independent of the choice of the oriented basis.

This is equivalent to saying that for any \(h\in SO(V) \), \( h^\# \) preserves \( q \). \[ \xymatrix{ \Pin(V) \ar@{->}[r]^{h^\#} \ar@{->}[d] & \Pin(V) \ar@{->}[d] \\ O(V) \ar@{->}[r]^{Ad(h)} & O(V) } \] Since image of \( q \) through vertical arrows is \( (-1)^{n+1} \), therefore preserved by any bottom horizontal arrow \( Ad(h) \), we know that \( h^\# \) sends \( q \) to \( \{\pm q\} \). It remains to evoke connectedness of \( SO(V) \) and the fact that \( {\rm Id}_{\Pin(V)}={\rm Id}_V^\# \) preserves \( q \).

Define \( q_0:=i^{\lfloor \frac{n+1}{2}\rfloor}q \) then \( q_0^2=1 \).

If \( n \) is odd, then \( q \) commutes with the entire \( \Cl(V) \). If \( n \) is even, \( q \) commutes with \( \Cl_0(V) \) and anticommutes with \( \Cl_1(V) \). Idem for \( q_0 \).

The eigenspaces corresponding to eigenvalues \( \pm 1 \) of \( q_0 \) as linear map on \(\Cl(V)\otimes \mathbb{C} \) are denoted by \( (\Cl(V)\otimes \mathbb{C})^\pm \), i.e. \[ \Cl(V)\otimes \mathbb{C} \simeq (\Cl(V)\otimes \mathbb{C})^+\oplus (\Cl(V)\otimes \mathbb{C})^- \] This is a decomposition of \( \mathbb{C} \)-vector spaces and, in addition if \( n \) is odd, a decomposition of \( \mathbb{C} \)-algebra.

  1. When \( n \) is even, the previous decomposition is not multiplicative. However, it is the case when one restricts to \(\Cl_0(2m)\otimes \mathbb{C}\subset \Cl(2m)\otimes \mathbb{C} \), i.e. the decomposition \[ \Cl_0(2m)\otimes \mathbb{C} \simeq (\Cl_0(2m)\otimes \mathbb{C})^+\oplus (\Cl_0(2m)\otimes \mathbb{C})^- \] is among \( \mathbb{C} \)-algebras.
  2. Moreover the two decompositions coincide via the map \( \Cl_0(2m)\simeq \Cl(2m-1) \), i.e. \[ \xymatrix{ \Cl_0(2m)\otimes\mathbb{C} \ar@{->}[r] \ar@{->}[d] & (\Cl_0(2m)\otimes \mathbb{C})^{\pm} \ar@{->}[d] \ar@/^/@{->}[l] \\ \Cl(2m-1)\otimes\mathbb{C} \ar@{->}[r] & (\Cl(2m-1)\otimes\mathbb{C})^{\pm} \ar@/^/@{->}[l] } \]

One has the following isomorphisms of \( \mathbb{C} \)-algebras:

  1. \( \Cl(2m)\otimes \mathbb{C}\simeq M(\mathbb{C}, 2^m) \),
  2. \( \Cl(2m+1)\otimes \mathbb{C}\simeq M(\mathbb{C},2^m)\oplus M(\mathbb{C}, 2^m) \),

where \( M(\mathbb{C},k) \) denotes the algebra of square matrices of size \( k \).

By induction, using

\begin{align*} \Cl(V\oplus \mathbb{R}^2)\otimes \mathbb{C} &\stackrel{\simeq}{\longrightarrow} (\Cl(V\oplus \mathbb{R}^2)\otimes \mathbb{C})\otimes(\Cl(\mathbb{R}^2)\otimes\mathbb{C})\\ e_1, e_2 &\longmapsto 1\otimes e_1, 1\otimes e_2\\ v &\longmapsto i v\otimes e_1e_2 \end{align*}

where \( \{e_i\} \) is an orthonormal basis of \( \mathbb{R}^2 \) and \( \{v\} \) is an orthonormal basis of \( V \). As for the base case,

  1. \( \Cl(1)\simeq \mathbb{C} \) so \( \Cl(1)\otimes \mathbb{C}\simeq \mathbb{C}\oplus \mathbb{C} \),
  2. \( \Cl(2)\simeq \mathbb{H} \) so \( \Cl(2)\otimes \mathbb{C}\simeq \mathbb{H}\otimes \mathbb{C}\simeq M(\mathbb{C},2) \).

Another way to see that \(\Cl(2m+1)\otimes \mathbb{C}\simeq M(\mathbb{C},2^m)\oplus M(\mathbb{C}, 2^m) \) is by noticing that

  1. the decomposition of Definition def:split-cl is a decomposition of \( \mathbb{C} \)-algebra when \( n \) is odd.
  2. \(\Cl_0(2m+1)\otimes \mathbb{C}\simeq (\Cl(2m+1)\otimes \mathbb{C})^{\pm} \) as \( \mathbb{C} \)-algebra via \( v\longmapsto \frac{v+q_0v}{2} \) which is injective (hence bijective by dimension) because \( q_0\in \Cl_1(2m+1)\otimes \mathbb{C} \). This map is multiplicative because \( q_0 \) commutes with \( \Cl_0(2m+1) \).

Composing the maps, one has

\begin{align*} \Cl(2m+1)\otimes \mathbb{C} &\simeq (\Cl(2m+1)\otimes \mathbb{C})^+\oplus(\Cl(2m+1)\otimes \mathbb{C})^-\\ &\simeq (\Cl_0(2m+1)\otimes \mathbb{C})\oplus (\Cl_0(2m+1)\otimes \mathbb{C}) \\ &\simeq (\Cl(2m)\otimes \mathbb{C})\oplus(\Cl(2m)\otimes \mathbb{C}) \end{align*}

Keeping track of the composition, one has

\begin{align*} \Cl(2m+1)\otimes \mathbb{C} &\stackrel{\simeq}{\longrightarrow} (\Cl(2m)\otimes \mathbb{C})\oplus(\Cl(2m)\otimes \mathbb{C})\\ v &\longmapsto \restr{(v+q_0 v)_0}{e_{2m+1} = 1 } \oplus \restr{(v-q_0 v)_0}{e_{2m+1} = 1 } \\ \frac{u_1 + u_2 + q_0(u_1 - u_2)}{2} &\longleftarrow\!\shortmid u_1 \oplus u_2 \end{align*}

where

  • \( v\mapsto v_0 \) denotes the projection to \( \Cl_0 \) and
  • \( v\mapsto \restr{v}{e_{2m+1}=1} \) is the inverse of the map in Remark rem:cl-first, i.e. "push" \( e_{2m+1} \) in the last, then replace it by 1.

Since \( q_0 \in Cl(2m+1) \) when \( m \) is even, one has

For any \( m\geq 1 \), \[ \Cl(2m+1)\otimes \mathbb{C} \simeq (\Cl(2m)\otimes \mathbb{C})\oplus(\Cl(2m)\otimes \mathbb{C}) \] In addition, if \( m \) is even, one can remove all tensor product with \( \mathbb{C} \).

This will later help us to see that \( \Cl(3)\simeq \mathbb{H}\oplus \mathbb{H} \).

The complex Spin group \( \Spin^c(V) \) is the subgroup of \( (\Cl(V)\otimes \mathbb{C})^\times \) generated by \( \Spin(V) \) and \( \{z\in \mathbb{C}: |z|=1 \} \).

In other words, as a group \( \Spin^c(V) \simeq \Spin(V)\times_{\pm}U(1) \) where \( \mathbb{Z}/2 \mathbb{Z} \) acts on \( U(1) \) by \( e^{i\theta}\mapsto e^{-i\theta} \).

The following commutative diagram clarify what we mean when we say that \( \Spin^c(n) \) is a double cover of \( SO(n)\times U(1) \) and \( \Spin(n)\times U(1) \) is a double cover of \( \Spin^c(n) \). \[ \xymatrix{ (s,z) \ar@{|->}[r] & (\pm s, z) \\ \Spin(n)\times U(1) \ar@{->}[r]^{2:1} \ar@{->}[d]_{2:1} & SO(n)\times U(1) \ar@{->}[d]^{2:1} \\ \Spin^c(n) \ar@{->}[r]^{2:1} & SO(n)\times U(1) \\ \pm(s,z) \ar@{|->}[r] & (\pm s, z^2) } \]

To compute the fundamental group of \( \Spin^c(n) \), one can use the following exact sequence: \[ \xymatrix{ 0 \ar@{->}[r] & \Spin(n) \ar@{->}[r]_{{w\mapsto \pm(w,1)}} & \Spin^c(n) \ar@{->}[r]_{{\pm(w,z)\mapsto z^2}} & U(1) \ar@{->}[r] & 0 } \] One see that \( \pi_1(\Spin^c(n))=\mathbb{Z} \) because \[ \xymatrix{ 0 \ar@{->}[r] & 0 \ar@{->}[r] & \pi_1(\Spin^c(n)) \ar@{->}[r] & \mathbb{Z} \ar@{->}[r] & 0 } \]

It is also useful to mention the following exact sequence \[ \xymatrix{ 0 \ar@{->}[r] & U(1) \ar@{->}[r]_{{z\mapsto \pm(1,z)}} & \Spin^c(n) \ar@{->}[r]_{{\pm(w,z) \mapsto\pm w}} & SO(n) \ar@{->}[r] & 0 } \] which gives \[ \xymatrix{ 0 \ar@{->}[r] & \mathbb{Z} \ar@{->}[r] & \pi_1(\Spin^c(n)) \ar@{->}[r] & \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \ar@{->}[r] & 0 } \]

2.1.3 Spin representation and its decomposition

Proposition prop:alg-str-cl and the following fact tells us all irreducible complex representation of \( \Cl(V) \)

The \(\mathbb{C}\)-algebra \( M(\mathbb{C},k) \) has a unique irreducible \( \mathbb{C} \)-representation \( S_{\mathbb{C}}(k) \) and it has dimension \( k \).

We can conclude that there is only one irreducible complex representation \(S_{\mathbb{C}}(2m) \) of \( \Cl(2m)\otimes \mathbb{C} \), called the Spin representation. On the other hand, \( \Cl(2m+1)\otimes \mathbb{C} \) has two irreducible complex representations given by having one factor \( M(\mathbb{C},2^m) \) acting nontrivially on \( S_{\mathbb{C}}(2m) \) and the other acts trivially.

  1. The representation \( S_{\mathbb{C}}(2m) \) of \( \Cl(2m)\otimes \mathbb{C} \) splits by action of \( q_0 \) \[ S_{\mathbb{C}}(2m) \stackrel{\simeq}{\longrightarrow} S_{\mathbb{C}}(2m)^+ \oplus S_{\mathbb{C}}(2m)^- \] not only as \( \mathbb{C} \)-vector space, but also as \( \Cl_0(2m)\otimes \mathbb{C} \)-module.
  2. Action by multiplication of \( \Cl_1(2m) \) swaps the 2 subspaces. Moreover the decomposition of the action of an element of \( \Cl_1(2m)\otimes \mathbb{C} \) to its restrictions on \( S_{\mathbb{C}}(2m)^{\pm} \longrightarrow S_{\mathbb{C}}(2m)^{\mp} \) is exactly the decomposition \( \Cl_1(2m)\otimes \mathbb{C} \simeq (\Cl_1(2m)\otimes \mathbb{C})^+\oplus (\Cl_1(2m)\otimes \mathbb{C})^- \), i.e. \[ \xymatrix@C=5pc{ \Cl_1(2m)\otimes \mathbb{C} \ar@{->}[r]^>>>>>>>>>>{{w\mapsto (w,w)}} \ar@{->}[rd]_<<<<<<<<<<<{{w\mapsto (w^-, w^+)}} & \Hom_{\mathbb{C}}(S_{\mathbb{C}}^+,S_{\mathbb{C}}^-)\oplus\Hom_{\mathbb{C}}(S_{\mathbb{C}}^-,S_{\mathbb{C}}^+) \\ & (\Cl_1(2m)\otimes \mathbb{C})^+\oplus (\Cl_1(2m)\otimes \mathbb{C})^- \ar@{->}[u]_{{(w^-,w^+)\mapsto (w^-,w^+)}} } \] where all arrows are \( \mathbb{C} \)-vector space isomorphisms.
  3. The subalgebra \( \Cl_0(2m) \) acts separately on \( S_{\mathbb{C}}^{\pm}(2m) \) and its restriction on \( S_{\mathbb{C}}(2m)^{\pm} \longrightarrow S_{\mathbb{C}}(2m)^{\pm} \) is exactly the decomposition \( \Cl_0(2m)\otimes \mathbb{C} \simeq (\Cl_0(2m)\otimes \mathbb{C})^+\oplus (\Cl_0(2m)\otimes \mathbb{C})^- \), i.e. \[ \xymatrix@C=5pc{ \Cl_0(2m)\otimes \mathbb{C} \ar@{->}[r]^>>>>>>>>>>{{w\mapsto (w,w)}} \ar@{->}[rd]_<<<<<<<<<<<{{w\mapsto (w^-, w^+)}} & \End_{\mathbb{C}}(S_{\mathbb{C}}^+)\oplus\End_{\mathbb{C}}(S_{\mathbb{C}}^-) \\ & (\Cl_0(2m)\otimes \mathbb{C})^+\oplus (\Cl_0(2m)\otimes \mathbb{C})^- \ar@{->}[u]_{{(w^-,w^+)\mapsto (w^-,w^+)}} } \] where all arrows are \( \mathbb{C} \)-algebra isomorphism.
  1. \( S_{\mathbb{C}} (2m)^{\pm}\) are the only irreducible representations of \( \Spin(2m)\) that extend to (automatically irreducible) representation of \( \Cl_0(2m) \).
  2. \( S_{\mathbb{C}} (2m)\) is irreducible representation of \( \Spin(2m+1) \) that extends to (automatically irreducible) representation of \( \Cl_0(2m+1) \).

2.2 Examples and computations in 4 dimensions

It is clear that \( \Cl(1) = \mathbb{R}[i]/_{(i^2+1)}=\mathbb{C} \). So \( \Cl(1)\otimes \mathbb{C} = \mathbb{C}\oplus \mathbb{C} \).

Our strategy for the next computations will be as follows:

  • Suppose we already know \( \Cl(2) \) and its complex representation \( S_{\mathbb{C}}(2) \).
  • We can compute \( \Cl(3)\simeq \Cl(2)\oplus\Cl(2) \) using Corollary cor:cl-incr-1. Its representation will be \( S_\mathbb{C}(2)\oplus S_{\mathbb{C}}(2) \) where each factor acts separately.
  • Knowing \( \Cl(3) \) means knowing \( \Cl_0(4) \). By Remark rem:cl0-decomp, \( \Cl_0(4) = \Cl_0(4)^+\oplus \Cl_0(4)^- \) (we do not need to complexify because \( q_0\in \Cl(4) \)) coincides with \( \Cl(3)=\Cl(3)^+\oplus \Cl(3)^-=\Cl(2)\oplus\Cl(2) \). Apply Proposition prop:spin-rep-split, we see that \( S_{\mathbb{C}}^\pm(4) \) are complex representations of \( \Cl(2) \), so we can identify \( \Cl_0(4) = \Cl(2)\oplus\Cl(2) \) and \( S_{\mathbb{C}}(4) = S_{\mathbb{C}}(2)\oplus S_{\mathbb{C}}(2) \).

2.2.1 Representation of \( \Cl(2)\otimes \mathbb{C} \).

The \( \mathbb{R} \)-algebra \( \Cl(2) \) is isomorphic to \( \mathbb{H}=\mathbb{R}\oplus\mathbb{R}I\oplus \mathbb{R}J\oplus \mathbb{R}K\) via \( e_1, e_2\mapsto J,K \). The following Lemma computes \( \Cl(2)\otimes \mathbb{C} \) and the Spin representation.

The \( \mathbb{R} \)-algebra \( \mathbb{H} \) of quaternions is isomorphic to \[ \mathbb{H}\simeq \left\{ \begin{bmatrix} a & -\bar b \\ b & \bar a \end{bmatrix}: a,b\in \mathbb{C}\right\} \] where

\begin{equation} \label{eq:rep-H} I:= \begin{bmatrix} i & 0 \\ 0 & -i \end{bmatrix},\qquad J:= \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix},\qquad K:= \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -i \\ -i & 0 \end{bmatrix},\qquad \end{equation}

This \(\mathbb{R}\)-algebra of matrices is not a \( \mathbb{C} \)-vector space and can be complexified by adding \[ \sqrt{-1}:=\begin{bmatrix} i & 0 \\ 0 & i \end{bmatrix} \] which gives \( \mathbb{H}\otimes \mathbb{C}=M(\mathbb{C},2) \).

Moreover, \eqref{eq:rep-H} is the representation of \( \mathbb{H} \) on \( \mathbb{C}\oplus J\mathbb{C}\) where \( \mathbb{C}=R[I]/_{(I^2+1)} \).

Since \( q_0 = \sqrt{-1}e_1e_2 = \sqrt{-1}:=\begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \) and \[ \Spin(2) = \left\{ \cos\theta +\sin\theta e_1e_2 = \begin{bmatrix} e^{i\theta} & 0 \\ 0 & e^{-i\theta} \end{bmatrix}\right\} \simeq U(1), \] one has

The Spin representation in dimension 2 is \[ S_{\mathbb{C}}(2) = \mathbb{C}\oplus J\mathbb{C} = S_{\mathbb{C}}^-(2) \oplus S_{\mathbb{C}}^+(2) \] where \( S_{\mathbb{C}}^-(2) = \mathbb{C} \) and \( S_{\mathbb{C}}^+(2) = J \mathbb{C} \).

2.2.2 Representation of \( \Cl(3)\otimes \mathbb{C} \).

By Corollary cor:cl-incr-1, \( \Cl(3)\simeq \Cl(2)\oplus \Cl(2)\simeq \mathbb{H}\oplus \mathbb{H}\) as \( \mathbb{R} \)-algebra and the isomorphism is given by \[ e_1 \mapsto (K,-K),\qquad e_2\mapsto (-J,J),\qquad e_3\mapsto (I,-I) \] and \( q_0 = -e_1e_2e_3\mapsto (1,-1) \) as it should be.

Therefore, one has

  1. \( \Cl_0(3) \subset \Cl(3)\simeq \mathbb{H}\oplus \mathbb{H} \) is the diagonal
  2. The Pin group is \[ \Pin(3) = \langle (q,-q): q\in \im \mathbb{H} \rangle \]
  3. The Spin group is (after a quick computation): \[ \Spin(3) = \langle (q,q): q\in \mathbb{H}, |q|=1 \rangle \simeq Sp(1)\simeq SU(2) \] where \( Sp(1) = \{q\in \mathbb{H}: |q|=1\} \) is the unit sphere in \( \mathbb{H} \), which can be seen by Lemma lem:rep-H to be isomorphic to \( SU(2) \).
  4. The Spin representation, as seen by Corollary cor:spin-rep-split, is given by action of \( \Spin(3)\subset \Cl_0(3) \) (as diagonal of \( \mathbb{H}\oplus \mathbb{H} \)) on \( \mathbb{H}\simeq \mathbb{C}^2 \).
