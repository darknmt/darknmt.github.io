** Changement de base
\[
X(A)\times Y(A) = (X\times_k Y)(A)
\]
pour tout \( k \)-schema \( A,X,Y \).
** Fibre schématique
   Soit \( X \) un schéma sur \( Y \).
\[
X_y := X\times_Y \Spec k(y)
\]
entant que espace topologique. D'où une structure de schéma sur \( X_y \).
** Propriétés préservées par le changement de base


#+ATTR_HTML: :id thm:prop-preserve-changement-base :btit
#+BEGIN_theorem
#+LATEX: \label{thm:prop-preserve-changement-base}
Soit \( Y' \) un schéma sur \( Y \), alors les propriétés suivantes de \( f:\ X
\longrightarrow Y \) sont préservées par le morphisme induit \( \tilde f:\ X\times_Y Y'
\longrightarrow Y' \):
1. immersion ouverte, immersion fermée,
2. localement de type fini, de type fini,
3. quasi-compact,
4. fini,
5. affine.
#+END_theorem

** Propriété locale
#+BEGIN_definition
Une propriété \( P \) est dite _locale_ si la définition de \( P \) a priori établit sur
un recouvrement reste valable pour tout ouvert.

Soit \( f: X \longrightarrow  Y \) un morphisme de schémas. Une propriété \( P \) de \( f
\) est dite _locale sur la base_ si les suivants sont équivalents
1. \( f \) vérifie \( P \).
2. \( Y \) admet un recouvrement affine \( Y = \cup V_i \) tq \(\restr{f}{f^{-1}(V_i)}:\ f^{-1}(V_i) \longrightarrow V_i \) vérifie \( P \).
#+END_definition


#+ATTR_LATEX: :options [schéma localement notherien]
#+ATTR_HTML: :id thm:loc-noetherian :btit (schéma localement notherien) 
#+BEGIN_theorem
#+LATEX: \label{thm:loc-noetherian}
Les énoncés suivants sont équivalents et définissent un schéma _localement noethérien_
1. Il existe un recouvrement affine \( X = \cup_{i\in I} U_i \) avec \( U_i = \Spec(A_i) \) et \(
   A_i \) sont noethériens.
2. Pour tout ouvert affine \( \Spec A = U\subset X \), \( A \) est noethérien.
Si \( I \) est fini, on dit que \( A \) est _noethérien_.
#+END_theorem

*** Morphisme quasi-compact

#+BEGIN_definition
Un morphisme \( f:\ X \longrightarrow Y \) est dit _quasi-compact_ si pour tout ouvert
affine \( V\subset Y \), \( f^{-1}(V) \) est quasi-compact.
#+END_definition

#+ATTR_LATEX: :options [morphisme quasi-compact]

#+BEGIN_theorem
#+LATEX: \label{thm:mor-quasi-compact}
La quasi-compacité d'un morphisme est une propriété locale sur la base.
#+END_theorem

#+ATTR_LATEX: :options [localement de type fini]
#+ATTR_HTML: :id thm:loc-type-fini :btit (localement de type fini) 
#+BEGIN_theorem
#+LATEX: \label{thm:loc-type-fini}
Soit \( f: X \longrightarrow Y \) un morphisme de schémas. Les suivants sont équivalents
et rendent \( f \) un morphisme _localement de type fini_:
1. Pour tout ouvert affine \( V\subset Y \) et tout ouvert affine \( U\subset f^{-1}(Y)
   \), \( \mathcal{O}_X(U) \) est une \( \mathcal{O}_Y(V) \) algèbre de type fini.
2. Il existe un recouvrement affine \( Y = \cup_i V_i \) tq \( f^{-1}(V_i) = \cup_j U_{ij}
   \) où \( \mathcal{O}_X(V_{ij}) \) est un \( \mathcal{O}_Y(V_i) \) algèbre de type fini.
Si de plus \( f \) est quasi-compact, on dit qu'il est _de type fini_.
#+END_theorem

#+BEGIN_proposition
1. La composée de deux morphisme de type fini/quasi-compact/loc. de type fini est de type
   fini/quasi-compact/loc. de type fini.
2. Si \( f\circ g \) est localement de type fini, alors \( f \) et \( g \) sont localement
   de type fini.
#+END_proposition

#+BEGIN_exampl
Une immersion ouvert est localement de type fini. La raison est que \( A_f \) est une \( A
\)-algẻbre de type fini.
#+END_exampl

#+BEGIN_definition
1. Un morphisme \( f: \ X \longrightarrow Y \) est dit _affine_ si \( f^{-1}(V) \) est
   affine \( \forall V\subset Y \) ouvert affine.
2. \( f \) est dit _fini_ s'il est affine et \( \mathcal{O}_X(f^{-1}(V)) \) est une \(
   \mathcal{O}_Y(V) \) algèbre de type fini.
#+END_definition

* Algebraic Geometry - Lecture 3: <2017-10-23 Mon>

Here are some subtle points in the Lecture 3.

** Proof of $\dim = \deg h_K$: Algebraic and Geometric interpretation of intersection

The idea of the proof is to use recurrence on \(\deg h_K\). For the case $\deg h_K = 0$, it
was proposed as an exercise to prove that $X$ is affine. Then $X$ is an finite
dimensional \(k\)-algebra where prime ideals are maximal. The reduction part is base on a
/good intersection/ of the projective scheme $X$ with a hyperplan defined by $f$.

The geometric condition for a /good intersection/ is that the hyperplan does not contain
any top dimensional irreducible component, i.e. 
\[
f\not\in \bigcup_{p \text{ minimal prime }}p
\]
so that the intersection is of dimension decreasing at least 1.

The corresponding algebraic condition is that \( f \) is not a divisor of \( 0 \), so that
one can apply the following result.

#+ATTR_LATEX: :options [Krull principal ideal theorem]
#+ATTR_HTML: :id thm:krull-principal :btit (Krull principal ideal theorem) 
#+BEGIN_theorem
#+LATEX: \label{thm:krull-principal}
Let \( A \) be a noetherian ring, \( f\in A \) and \( p \) is a minimal prime ideal among
those containing \( f \). Then \( ht(p) \leq 1 \), in fact it is \( 0 \) if \( p \)
divises \( 0 \) and \( 1 \) if not.
#+END_theorem

#+BEGIN_remark
One can see that \( \bigcup_{p \text{ minimal prime }}\subset \{ \text{divisors of zero}\}
\) from the more general point of view of /associated prime ideals/. So the algebraic
condition is stricter than the geometric one.
#+END_remark


** \( K \)-points of a scheme are closed if \( K/k \) is algebraic
The \( K \)-points of a scheme \( X \) over a field \( k \) are defined as the images of
\( \Spec K \) in \( X \) as \( k \)-scheme. Equivalently, it means that the /residual
field/ \( k(x) = \mathcal{O}_{X,x}/\mathfrak{m}_x \) is a subfield of \( K \).

If \( K/k \) is an algebraic extension then these are closed points. This is equivalent to
the fact that such points are closed in _every_ affine open sets of \( X \).

Let \( U=\Spec A \) be an open set containing a \( k \)-point \( p \), denoted as a prime
ideal of \( A \), then the residual field at \( p \) is \( Frac(A/p) \) the fraction field
of an integral domain \( A/p \) algebraic over \( k \). This also means that \( A/p \) is
actually a field, hence \( p \) is maximal and therefore closed in \( U \).

** Proof of locality of "local noetherian" property

To prove that if a schema \( X \) is a union of noetherian affine scheme then every affine
open set of \( X \) is noetherian, i.e. the property of being local noetherian is a local
property, one reduces the to the following context: \( A \) is a affine scheme cover by
noetherian affine open set \( \Spec B_i \) then \( A \) is noetherian. The idea is to use
quasi-compactness to reduce to the case where \( B_i = A_{f_i} \) and \( i \) belongs to a
finite set.

The subtle point is that if \( \Spec B \subset \Spec A \) than a set \( D(f) \) in \( \Spec
A \) is not neccessarily of form \( D(g) \) in \( \Spec B \). The additional data used
here is that not only \( \Spec B_i \longrightarrow \Spec A \) but this inclusion is also a
morphism between schemes, one therefore can recover the ring morphism \( A \longrightarrow B \).

#+BEGIN_remark
The lesson is that an affine open subset of a scheme \( X \) is not just an open subset
that is affine. It is the image of an affine scheme in \( X \).
#+END_remark
